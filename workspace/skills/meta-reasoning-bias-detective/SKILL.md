# Meta-Reasoning & Bias Detective

## Purpose
Enhance reasoning quality by identifying and addressing cognitive biases, logical fallacies, hidden assumptions, and reasoning blind spots in thought processes, both human and AI, through structured meta-cognitive analysis and self-reflection frameworks.

## Key Responsibilities
1. Bias Detection: Identify systematic thinking errors and cognitive biases
2. Fallacy Identification: Recognize logical fallacies in arguments and reasoning chains
3. Assumption Surfacing: Make implicit assumptions explicit and testable
4. Reasoning Audit: Review thought processes for soundness and completeness
5. Perspective Expansion: Introduce alternative viewpoints and considerations
6. Uncertainty Calibration: Help accurately assess confidence levels
7. Argument Reconstruction: Break down arguments into component premises
8. Evidence Evaluation: Assess quality and relevance of supporting evidence

## Cognitive Bias Categories

### Information Processing Biases
- Confirmation Bias: Seeking information that confirms existing beliefs
- Availability Heuristic: Overweighting easily recalled information
- Anchoring Bias: Over-relying on first piece of information encountered
- Representativeness Heuristic: Judging probability by surface similarities
- Gambler's Fallacy: Expecting past random events to affect future outcomes
- Base Rate Neglect: Ignoring prior probability in favor of specific information
- Conjunction Fallacy: Believing combined events more likely than single events
- Disjunction Fallacy: Underestimating possibility of at least one event occurring

### Memory & Recall Biases
- Hindsight Bias: Overestimating ability to predict past events
-rospect theory: Asymmetric valuation of gains vs. losses
- Sunk Cost Fallacy: Continuing based on past investment rather than future value
- Peak-End Rule: Judging experiences by peak and ending moments
- Duration Neglect: Ignoring length of experiences in overall assessment
- Serial Position Effect: Better recall for first and last items
- Misinformation Effect: Post-event information distorting memory

### Decision & Choice Biases
- Loss Aversion: Stronger response to losses than equivalent gains
- Endowment Effect: Overvaluing items one owns
- Status Quo Bias: Preferring current state over changes
- Framing Effects: Different responses based on problem presentation
- Contrast Effect: Judging values relative to recent exposures
- Compromise Effect: Preferring middle options in a range
- Attraction Effect: New options change perceived attractiveness of others
- Decoy Effect: Introducing irrelevant option to change preferences

### Belief & Reasoning Biases
- Dunning-Kruger Effect: Overestimating competence in low-skill areas
- Illusion of Control: Overestimating one's influence over outcomes
- Fundamental Attribution Error: Over-attributing behavior to disposition
- Self-Serving Bias: Attributing successes to self, failures to external
- Optimism Bias: Underestimating likelihood of negative events
- Self-fulfilling Prophecy: Beliefs influence outcomes to confirm beliefs
- Authority Bias: Overweighting authority figure opinions
- Bandwagon Effect: Doing things because others do them
- Halo Effect: Overall impression influences specific trait judgments
- Horns Effect: Negative trait influences overall positive judgment

### Group & Social Biases
- Groupthink: Suppressing dissent for apparent group harmony
- Abilene Paradox: Collective agreement contrary to individual preferences
- Spiral of Silence: Staying quiet when one's views seem minority
- Echo Chamber Effect: Only hearing confirming perspectives
- In-group/Out-group Bias: Favoring own group over others
- Collective Tunneling: Group focus on narrow issues excluding broader context

## Logical Fallacy Types

### Formal Fallacies
- Affirming Consequent: If A then B; B therefore A
- Denying Antecedent: If A then B; not A therefore not B
- Affirming Disjunct: A or B; A therefore not B
- Undistributed Middle: All A are B; all C are A; therefore all C are B
- Illicit Major/Minor: Distributed term issue in categorical syllogisms
- Negative Conclusion from Affirmative Premises: Contradictory conclusions
- Four-Term Fallacy: Introducing a fourth term in syllogism

### Informal Fallacies
- Ad Hominem: Attacking arguer rather than argument
- Straw Man: Misrepresenting opponent's position
- False Dilemma/False Dichotomy: Presenting only two options when more exist
- Slippery Slope: Small step leads to extreme undesirable outcome
- Circular Reasoning/Begging the Question: Conclusion in premise
- Ad Ignorantiam: Claim true because not disproven
- Appeal to Authority: Authority figure opinion as evidence
- Appeal to Emotion: Emotional manipulation as argument
- Appeal to Tradition: Something is right because traditional
- Appeal to Novelty: Something is right because new
- Red Herring: Irrelevant information distracting from issue
- Tu Quoque: Criticizing accuser rather than addressing accusation
- Genetic Fallacy: Rejecting based on origin rather than merit
- Middle Ground Fallacy: Compromise must be correct
- Hasty Generalization: Small sample leading to broad claim
- Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc: Correlation mistaken for causation
- Cum Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc: Events occurring together implies causation
- Sweeping Generalization: General rule applied where doesn't apply
- Joint Variation Fallacy: Assuming cause when only correlation exists

## Meta-Reasoning Framework

### Self-Awareness Prompts
1. What am I assuming that could be wrong?
2. What evidence would change my mind?
3. What do I not know that I should find out?
4. What would the opposite position argue?
5. How confident am I and why?
6. What biases might be affecting my judgment?
7. Am I simplifying complex issues?
8. What are the second and third-order effects?
9. Is this a known pattern or novel situation?
10. What would an expert in this domain say?

### Reasoning Structure Analysis
- Premise Identification: What are the foundational claims?
- Inference Chains: Are conclusions logically derived?
- Evidence Quality: How strong is the supporting evidence?
- Assumption Audit: Are assumptions justified and tested?
- Alternative Paths: What other conclusions are possible?
- Uncertainty Quantification: How robust are the conclusions?

### Perspective-Taking Techniques
- Steel Man: Construct strongest version of opposing view
- Devil's Advocate: Systematically argue against your position
- 10-10-10 Rule: How would I feel about this in 10 minutes/hours/days?
- Pre-Mortem Analysis: Imagine failure and work backward to causes
- Outside View: What would similar situations suggest about this?
- First Principles: What is objectively true about this?
- Inversion: What would be the opposite approach and why?

## Bias Mitigation Strategies

### For Individual Biases
1. Structured Decision Processes: checklists, frameworks
2. Pre-mortem Exercises: Defensive reasoning about potential failures
3. Pre-commitment Devices: Lock in decisions before emotions affect
4. Contingency Planning: Prepare for alternatives
5. Explicit Criteria: Document decision criteria before decisions
6. Red Teams: Hire有人 to challenge your thinking
7. Consider the Opposite: Deliberately examine counterevidence
8. Pause and Reflect: Insert delay between stimulus and response
9. Decompose Problems: Break into smaller, more manageable parts
10. Seek Disconfirming Evidence: Active search for contradictory information

### For Group Reasoning
1. Killer Stories: Someone explicitly assigned to find flaws
2. Pre-registration: Lock in analysis plans before seeing data
3. Blind Analysis: Remove identifying information before evaluation
4. Devil's Advocacy Rotation: Systematic role assignment
5. Prediction Markets: Betting on outcomes to surface collective wisdom
6. Delphi Method: Iterative anonymous expert input
7. Red Teams: Dedicated adversarial analysis groups
8. Structured Debates: Formal rules for presenting and challenging views

### For Organizational Processes
1. Decision Journals: Document reasoning and revisit outcomes
2. Pre-mortems on Projects: Systematic failure analysis
3. Success/Post-mortems: Learn from completed projects
4. Scenario Planning: Multiple futures considered
5. Redundancy & Diversity: Multiple approaches for critical decisions
6. Reversibility Consideration: Prefer reversible when possible
7. Timeout Mechanisms: Pause button for reconsidering decisions
8. Escalation Paths: Clear processes for raising concerns

## Calibration & Uncertainty

### Assessing Confidence
1. Frequency Estimation: How often have I been right about this?
2. Reference Class Forecasting: What do similar situations suggest?
3. Calibration Questions: Am I right 70% when I say 70% confident?
4. Decomposition: Break into knowable and unknowable components
5. Outside View: What do base rates suggest?
6. Expert Comparison: How does my view compare to experts?

### Expressing Uncertainty
- Point estimates with confidence intervals
- Probability distributions over outcomes
- Possibility vs. probability vs. plausibility
- Knightian uncertainty: Quantifiable vs. unquantifiable risk
- Scenario probabilities: Best case, expected, worst case
- Binary statements: Could happen / couldn't happen

## Reasoning Quality Assessment Checklist
- [ ] Are assumptions explicitly stated and justified?
- [ ] Is the logical chain from evidence to conclusion sound?
- [ ] Have alternative explanations been considered?
- [ ] Has contradictory evidence been sought and addressed?
- [ ] Is the confidence level appropriate to the evidence?
- [ ] Have relevant experts been consulted?
- [ ] Have base rates and prior probabilities been considered?
- [ ] Is the reasoning free from emotional contamination?
- [ ] Have second and third-order effects been considered?
- [ ] Would reasoning hold if the problem were framed differently?

## Collaboration Approach
- Ask for the reasoning or argument being evaluated
- Request underlying assumptions the person is making
- Seek the evidence supporting the conclusion
- Ask what information would change their mind
- Request consideration of alternative perspectives
- Probe for potential biases in information gathering
- Examine logical structure for fallacies
- Calibrate confidence based on evidence strength
- Suggest frameworks for more robust reasoning
- Encourage documentation of reasoning for later review
- Use Socratic questioning to surface hidden premises
- Provide alternative explanations without dismissal
