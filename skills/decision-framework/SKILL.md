# ⚖️ Decision Framework

Decision architect who replaces gut feelings with structured thinking. Not because intuition is wrong, but because writing it down reveals what your gut is actually saying.

## Process

1. **Clarify the decision** -- what exactly are you choosing between? Remove ambiguity. "Should I change jobs?" becomes "Should I accept the offer from Company X, or stay at Company Y?"
2. **Identify criteria** -- what matters? List every factor: financial, personal, career, lifestyle, risk. Include criteria you feel embarrassed about -- those matter most.
3. **Weight criteria** -- not everything matters equally. Distribute 100 points across criteria, or rank 1-10. Forces you to admit what you actually prioritize.
4. **Score options** -- rate each option against each criterion (1-5 scale). Score independently -- cover other columns while scoring.
5. **Analyze sensitivity** -- change the weights. Does the winner change? If small weight shifts flip the result, the decision is genuinely close and either option is defensible.
6. **Document decision and reasoning** -- write it down before you decide. Future you will thank present you, especially when evaluating outcomes.

## Framework Selection Guide

| Decision Type | Best Framework | When to Use |
|---------------|---------------|-------------|
| Simple binary (yes/no) | Pros/Cons with weighting | Two clear options, moderate stakes |
| Multi-option comparison | Weighted decision matrix | 3+ options, multiple criteria |
| Time-sensitive prioritization | Eisenhower matrix | Many tasks competing for attention |
| High-stakes irreversible | One-way/Two-way door | Need to gauge reversibility before depth of analysis |
| Group or consensus | Delphi method | Multiple stakeholders, reduce groupthink |

## Weighted Decision Matrix

**Example: Choosing between 3 job offers**

Criteria weights (must sum to 100):

| Criteria | Weight | Offer A (Startup) | Offer B (Corp) | Offer C (Remote) |
|----------|--------|--------------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Salary | 25 | 3 (75) | 5 (125) | 4 (100) |
| Growth potential | 30 | 5 (150) | 3 (90) | 4 (120) |
| Culture fit | 20 | 4 (80) | 2 (40) | 4 (80) |
| Location | 10 | 2 (20) | 3 (30) | 5 (50) |
| Work-life balance | 15 | 2 (30) | 4 (60) | 5 (75) |
| **Weighted Total** | **100** | **355** | **345** | **425** |

Scoring: 1 = poor, 5 = excellent. Weighted score = score x weight. Offer C wins, driven by balance and location -- which tells you those factors matter more than you might admit.

## Pros/Cons with Weighting

Standard pros/cons lists mislead because 3 minor pros look like they outweigh 1 critical con. Fix this by adding impact scores:

| Factor | Type | Impact (1-5) | Notes |
|--------|------|-------------|-------|
| Higher salary (+20%) | Pro | 4 | Significant lifestyle improvement |
| Shorter commute | Pro | 2 | Nice but not decisive |
| Better coffee machine | Pro | 1 | Irrelevant to decision |
| Lose vested equity | Con | 5 | 50k in unvested stock |
| **Pro total** | | **7** | |
| **Con total** | | **5** | |

The raw count says 3 pros vs 1 con. The weighted score reveals the con nearly outweighs all pros combined. Always weight.

## Cognitive Bias Checklist

Before finalizing, ask yourself these questions:

| Bias | Question to Ask Yourself |
|------|-------------------------|
| Sunk cost | "Would I still choose this if I hadn't already invested time/money?" |
| Status quo | "Am I choosing this because it's best, or because it's familiar?" |
| Anchoring | "Is my first impression or first number I heard unfairly influencing me?" |
| Confirmation bias | "Am I seeking information that supports what I already want to do?" |
| Loss aversion | "Am I overweighting what I might lose vs what I might gain?" |

## Anti-Patterns

- **Analysis paralysis** -- set a decision deadline. Imperfect action beats perfect indecision.
- **Equal weighting** -- giving everything the same weight defeats the entire purpose. Force yourself to differentiate.
- **Ignoring reversibility** -- two-way doors deserve less analysis, not more. Save the heavy frameworks for one-way doors.
- **Reverse-engineering the framework** -- building a matrix to justify the decision you already made. If you catch yourself adjusting weights until your favorite wins, stop and be honest.
- **Not recording for future learning** -- the decision journal only works if you come back to it. Set a calendar reminder.
